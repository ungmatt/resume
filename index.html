How eCommerce Transforms B2B Foodservice Distribution

By Matthew Ung Published 2025

In traditional B2B foodservice distribution, the ordering process is clunky: phone calls to order-takers, faxed orders, manual entry into the ERP, picking and packing, and constant back-and-forth on stock availability. It's slow, error-prone, and expensive.

The cost of manual ordering goes beyond time. Every manual entry is an opportunity for mistakes—wrong product code, wrong quantity, duplicate orders. These errors ripple through your warehouse, invoicing, and customer relationships. Customers get frustrated, your staff spends time fixing issues instead of delivering service, and you're leaving money on the table.

Why B2B eCommerce is different from B2C

B2B ordering has different rules. Your customers (restaurants, caterers, retailers) need bulk pricing, tiered discounts, account terms, and integration with their own systems. They're not buying one pack of chicken breast—they're placing orders of 50+ units with delivery schedules and custom pricing agreements.

A B2B platform that works lets your customers:

  • Self-serve ordering 24/7 — No more waiting for order-takers. Customers place orders on their schedule.
  • See live stock and delivery windows — Transparency builds confidence. No more "sorry, we're out"—they see what's available and plan accordingly.
  • Receive accurate, tiered pricing — Automatic discounts for volume, seasonal adjustments, promotional pricing—all applied correctly, every time.
  • Access order history and manage accounts — Easier reordering, better planning, less phone tag.
  • Integrate their systems with yours via API — For larger customers, direct system-to-system ordering reduces friction even further.

The business impact

When I led the eCommerce transition at Marrickville Freezers and later supported multiple member organizations at NAFDA, the results were consistent:

  • 25-40% reduction in order processing time — Less manual data entry, fewer errors, faster fulfillment.
  • Higher order frequency and basket size — When ordering is effortless, customers order more often and in larger quantities.
  • Improved customer retention — Customers who integrate with your ordering platform become stickier; they have skin in the game.
  • Better data for your business — Real-time visibility into customer demand, seasonality, and trends means you can forecast better and manage inventory more efficiently.
  • Reduced invoicing disputes — Fewer errors mean fewer arguments over what was ordered vs. what was delivered.

The catch: Integration matters more than the platform

Many foodservice distributors buy a shiny B2B eCommerce platform and then realize it's siloed—it doesn't talk to their ERP, their warehouse system, or their financial systems. Orders come in but still need manual intervention to fulfil. That's not a win.

The real value is integration. Your eCommerce platform must feed live product data, stock levels, and pricing to your customers in real-time. Customer orders must flow directly into your ERP and warehouse system for picking and fulfillment. Invoices and delivery confirmations must close the loop automatically.

This is where many projects fail—the platform vendor sells you a product, but nobody thinks about the integration work until it's too late. That's why selecting the right system and partner from day one matters.

Getting started: Questions to ask yourself

  • Are your order-takers spending more time on data entry than on customer service?
  • How much revenue are you leaving on the table due to manual ordering friction?
  • What would it be worth to have real-time inventory visibility for your customers?
  • Which of your customers would likely increase order frequency if it were easier?
  • How well does your current system integrate with a potential eCommerce platform?

If you're in the foodservice distribution space, eCommerce isn't a nice-to-have anymore—it's a competitive necessity. The right platform, properly integrated, transforms customer experience and operational efficiency.

ERP Solutions: Scaling Your Business Without Systems Getting in the Way

By Matthew Ung Published 2025

Growing a foodservice distribution business is hard enough. When your ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) system becomes the bottleneck instead of the enabler, it's even harder.

Many growing distributors face this crossroads: their legacy system (maybe System 77, Navision, or a spreadsheet-heavy setup) worked fine when you were smaller, but now it's limiting growth. Manual processes are choking the business. Reporting takes hours. Integration with eCommerce or vendors is a nightmare. It's time to move to something modern—but what?

The ERP landscape for foodservice distributors

If you're looking to scale, your main options are:

  • Microsoft Dynamics 365 Business Central — Cloud-native, affordable, integrates seamlessly with Microsoft 365, Office, and Power BI. Scales well for mid-market distributors ($20M–$500M revenue). Strong foodservice industry templates.
  • Dynamics NAV (on-premises) — If you're not ready for cloud but need something better than your current system. Mature, proven in distribution.
  • Industry-specific platforms — Systems like Epicor or NetSuite built specifically for foodservice. More expensive but deeper industry functionality.
  • Best-of-breed approach — Lightweight ERP core + specialized modules for WMS, BI, eCommerce, integrations. More complex but flexible.

Why most ERP migrations fail (and how to avoid it)

Here's what I see repeatedly: Companies pick an ERP for the features, then the implementation turns into a nightmare. Scope creeps. Data is messy. Timelines slip. Staff resist because nobody trained them properly. You launch with broken processes and a team that doesn't trust the system.

The real success factors aren't about the software—they're about the people and process.

  • Start with process clarity. Before you migrate, map your current state honestly. What works? What doesn't? What's just "how we've always done it"? Most successful migrations I've led involved some process improvement—not because the new system forced it, but because the new system gave us the chance to do it right.
  • Treat data migration like surgery, not a copy-paste. Your legacy system has years of accumulated cruft—obsolete products, closed vendors, bad master data. Migration is your chance to clean. Invest in data quality upfront; it saves months of pain after go-live.
  • Plan change management from day one. The best ERP fails if your team doesn't adopt it. That means training (not just watching videos), sponsorship from leadership, quick wins to build confidence, and ongoing support post-launch.
  • Integration architecture is not an afterthought. Your new ERP won't be an island. It needs to connect to your eCommerce platform, 3PL partners, accounting systems, reporting tools. Plan these integrations during vendor selection, not after.
  • Go live with a pilot, not a big bang. Pick your simplest product line or a subset of customers. Let the team build confidence. Work out the bugs. Then expand. Less risky, less stressful, better results.

The true cost of an ERP migration

When distributors ask me "How much does an ERP cost?", the answer is: more than the software license. Budget for:

  • Software licensing (usually 3-10% of project cost)
  • Implementation partner (35-50% of project cost) — this is where the real work happens
  • Infrastructure and cloud hosting (10-15% of project cost)
  • Data migration and cleanup (10-20% of project cost) — often underestimated
  • Training and change management (10-20% of project cost)
  • Contingency (10-20% of project cost) — for scope changes, issues, extensions

A typical mid-market ERP project costs $500K–$2M+ depending on scope and complexity. Not cheap, but compared to the cost of staying on a limiting system? The ROI is usually 18-36 months through reduced labor, better inventory management, improved customer service, and faster decision-making.

When to move, and when to wait

You should move if:

  • Your current system can't keep up with business volume or complexity.
  • Manual workarounds are eating up hours every week.
  • You're planning major growth and your system can't scale.
  • You need better data visibility and reporting for decision-making.
  • Your system is at end-of-life and vendor support is disappearing.

You might wait if:

  • Your business is stable and your system is working fine (don't fix what isn't broken).
  • You're in the middle of a major restructure or acquisition—wait for dust to settle.
  • You're understaffed. ERP projects require dedicated internal focus.
  • You haven't secured budget and executive sponsorship yet.

A strategic approach

When I work with distributors on ERP strategy, we start with the business roadmap: Where do you want to be in 3, 5, 10 years? What are the growth constraints? What do customers and staff struggle with today? Then we work backwards to identify which systems and integrations will remove those constraints.

Sometimes that's a full ERP replacement. Sometimes it's a best-of-breed approach. Sometimes it's optimizing your current system with better integrations and automation. The point is: the system serves the strategy, not the other way around.

If you're thinking about ERP, don't start with vendors—start with your business needs. Once you're clear on those, the right system becomes obvious. And that's where I can help: aligning your IT decisions with your business reality.
